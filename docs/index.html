Grocery Madness

A guide for savvy shopping in the East Bay

From Edith Matthias, Marquis Chambers, and Xavey Bzdek

The Context

We wanted to explore what grocery prices were like, right here in our own East Bay backyard. Right now, everything feels more expensive, and we hope that this project can provide locals with a snapshot of what their prices are doing.

In the U.S. Grocery increased 2.1% in the year of 2024. Egg prices are in the news everyday. Consumer are voicing their fears amidst looming agricultural tariffs. Everywhere people are talking about prices.

We looked at 3 Berkeley Grocery Stores and 3 Oakland Stores and compiled the prices of common food items in four major categories: Meat, Dairy, Wheat & Grains, Fruits & Vegetables

So how are your prices doing?

The Analysis

In this section, we are going through the data we collected in hopes of providing a better explanation of what these numbers could mean, as well as examining any trends we thought were interesting.

We'll start by looking at how meat prices compare from each of our stores, with a comparison of their Total Price.

Looking at the graph, the cheapest 2 stores we found were from Oakland, with the two Trader Joe's tying for thrid place, and Berkeley Bowl taking last place.

There was only about a $15 dollar gap between our cheapest and our most expensive store. Yet, within both cities, that same gap shrunk to about $7 in berkeley and $8 in Oakland.

The greatest explanation we could see for the diferences was in the price per pound of Chicken. Both Chicken Breast and Chicken Thighs were significatly cheaper in Grocery Outlet and in Foodmaxx, when compared to the Berkeley Grocery Stores options for the same products.

EGGS

So let's talk about eggs.

Expensive and troublesome to find, eggs have become a luxury item in the last few months due to an outbreak of bird flu that has caused prices to soar. The bird flu outbreak began in the U.S in 2022; since then, over 163 million chickens, turkeys, and other poultry birds have died. With the number of egg-laying birds massively depleted, egg supplies have been estimated to have decreased by 40% compared to last year. As a result, prices are almost double what they were in 2024.

And we could see the effects in our findings:

While overall dairy products were lower at Foodmaxx and Grocery Outlet, both stores had much higher prices for a dozen eggs, with eggs being $7.99 at Grocery Outlet and $8.99 at Foodmaxx. To contrast, smaller-scale stores like Trader Joe’s have managed to keep a dozen eggs under $5.

According to CNBC Make It, Trader Joe’s prevents their prices from spiking as they buy directly from suppliers as well as using private-label brands. This means that they avoid price markups, maintaining more control over costs. Trader Joe’s also recently put a limit on the number of eggs shoppers were able to purchase, limiting it to one dozen per customer to manage their limited supply.

The Produce

In today's world, finding fruits and vegetables is hardly a challenge, they're readily available at local grocery stores, farmers' markets, and even convenience stores. However, the true challenge lies in finding produce that meets the standards of quality many consumers are looking for and want.

High-quality fruits and vegetables, often grown without harmful pesticides or with sustainable practices, may not be as easily accessible in every store. And when you do find them, they tend to come with a higher price tag.

Here, we have all of our produce products compared so that shoppers can easily compare directly each of these products by price, and see what differences stand out to them.

Looking at the graph, Whole Foods appears to be an outlier. It stands tall above the rest, but the reason for that may be because Whole Foods specializes in Organic produce.

At all the Grocery Stores we examined, we took the non-organic options for our product baskets. This in turn may lead to some stores, such as Whole Foods, appearing much more expensive than they actually are.

This is where specialty stores like Berkeley Bowl stand out. Known for their incredible variety of fresh, high-quality produce, Berkeley Bowl prioritizes sourcing from local farmers and offering organic, non-GMO, and sustainably grown options, and it's still pretty cheap.

We spoke to Economist Dan Lindheim about what he thought of the local prices:

“Well, Berkeley bowl is not cheap, let's just be real. Berkeley bowl has incredible variety. It has incredible opportunities to buy lots of different things. Especially, if you know, fruits and vegetables, meat, things like that, but it's more of a quality store that isn't an expensive store.”

Berkeley Bowl ranked third in our Produce rankings, only $0.80 away from the cheapest store, Grocery Outlet. In our analysis, this category was more difficult to examine than the other three. For the most part that difficulty came from the variety of produce we found at each store. We found it hard to fairly compare the stores when we knew that each one chose to specialize in produce differently. But we wanted to share our findings either way.

The Reflection

Throughout the process of exploring this topic, we realized there were only so many things we could achieve.

Let’s take a look at where we fell short. In its beginning, this project had hoped to showcase the changes of food prices over time, an idea that was sparked by the White House announcing a plan to place 25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico, but during our time that tariff was never put into effect.

After that, we saw this project as a chance to take a deeper look into food prices as they were, and give consumers a little help with navigating the madness that exists in the vacuum of options that exist today.

Despite our careful analysis and best intentions, there were a few roadblocks.

The first was price gathering. We visited during a three day stretch all 6 of our stores to collect the data for this project. One immediate downside to this is that grocery store prices change day to day, even throughout the day in many cases. Another issue was to decide what prices to pick.

We decided to focus on the cheapest goods we could find, that were not organic, in order to remain consistent at each store. The drawback to that is potentially our analysis on the quality of certain products.

For example, in Whole Foods and Berkeley Bowl, many parts of the store default to organic products, as they are trying to specialize in those products specifically, more than they are trying to sell the cheapest goods they possibly can. That choice may make their non-organic products slightly more expensive than other stores, while simultaneously having cheaper organic options than other stores may have.

The second was calculations. In some cases we listed food by weight to keep our data consistent, yet certain stores sell products, particularly produce, by item, not by pound. An example of this is Trader Joe’s, where a yellow onion costs about $0.99 per onion, instead of listing its price per pound. In the data, we researched the weight of a typical Trader Joe’s Yellow onion and found it to be about 1 lb, so we simply kept the price of $0.99 per pound to represent their onions. Overall, the process did not create any stark outliers, but it is a part of the imperfections in our method.

Finally, we saw that occasionally stores were out of stock of items, but still had the price labels for those missing products. The biggest example was eggs.

At Whole Foods, we saw this first hand.

On this occasion, we returned to the store to see if the missing product had been replaced, and upon returning the next day, Whole Foods had indeed restocked its egg supply. Even in doing so, it is worth noting that each store we visited had very low egg supplies, even if their prices were reasonably unchanged.

Final Thoughts

We hope that this project can be helpful for all the Oakland/Berkeley shoppers out there, and we hope it gives people an easy way to explore what each store may have to offer, without diving too far into the madness themselves.

We also hope that everyone takes this opportunity to reflect on what this project is not: A full, in-depth, complete scope of the grocery posibilities in the East Bay, but instead a window into what already exists, a few places at a time.
